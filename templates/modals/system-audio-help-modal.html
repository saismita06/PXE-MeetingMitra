macOS doesn't let any browser capture system audio directly. The OS treats audio capture as a privileged operation that browsers (sandboxed apps) can't request. The standard workaround is a free virtual audio driver that routes the system output back into an input device the browser can read like a microphone.

Option A. Tab audio (no install)
  1. Open the source content in a tab (YouTube, Zoom web, etc.)
  2. Click System Audio in PXE MeetingMitra and pick the tab in the share dialog
  3. Tick Share tab audio before clicking Share

Works only for content playing in a browser tab. App audio (Slack, native Zoom client, etc.) won't be captured.

Option B. Virtual audio device (recommended)
  1. Install BlackHole 2ch (free). The installer signs in with no kernel-extension prompts.
  2. Open Audio MIDI Setup → create a Multi-Output Device containing your speakers + BlackHole. Set this as the system output.
  3. In PXE MeetingMitra, click Microphone and pick BlackHole 2ch as the input.

After this is set up, system audio captures continuously regardless of which tab/window is active.

Alternative paid tools: Loopback (Rogue Amoeba) is the most polished UX.

Windows is the easiest platform. Chrome and Edge can capture audio from any source the OS plays, including specific apps. No extra software needed.

System-wide capture
  1. Click System Audio or Mic + System in PXE MeetingMitra
  2. In the share dialog, pick Entire Screen
  3. Tick Share system audio (bottom-left of the dialog)
  4. Click Share
Per-window capture
  1. Pick Window instead of Entire Screen
  2. Choose the specific app's window
  3. Tick Share window audio

Captures only the chosen app's audio, useful for meeting recordings without your own keyboard/typing noise.

Stuck on "no audio track" errors? Some apps (Spotify desktop, DRM-protected video) suppress system-audio capture. Use a virtual cable like VB-Audio Cable as a workaround.

Linux has two distinct paths. Pick the one that matches what you actually want to capture:

Option A. Tab audio (no setup)
  1. Play the source content in a Chrome tab (YouTube, Zoom web, meeting URL, etc.)
  2. Click System Audio in PXE MeetingMitra, then pick that tab in the share dialog
  3. Tick Share tab audio before clicking Share
This won't show up in pavucontrol. Chrome captures tab audio internally and never routes through PulseAudio, so the Recording tab will say "No application is currently recording audio." That's normal: PXE MeetingMitra's meter is the source of truth.

Only captures audio playing inside a browser tab. Native apps (Slack, Spotify desktop, Discord) won't be picked up.

Option B. Switch source in pavucontrol mid-recording

Quick but limited: captures system audio at the cost of your mic input, useful only when you want to record what's playing through your headphones / speakers without your own voice.

  1. Click Microphone in PXE MeetingMitra
  2. Open pavucontrol → Recording tab
  3. Change PXE MeetingMitra's capture source from your mic to Monitor of <your output>
Option C. Expose a virtual source so Chrome will list it (recommended)

Why this is needed: Chrome on Linux deliberately filters "Monitor of …" sources out of the input device list it exposes to web apps, which is why PXE MeetingMitra's input-device picker doesn't show them even though pavucontrol does. The fix is to wrap a monitor source in a regular virtual source that Chrome doesn't filter. Once you do this, the new source appears in PXE MeetingMitra's picker and you can use it as either the primary input or as a "Also mix in" alongside your real mic to capture both your voice and system audio in one stream.

Quick setup (one-time)

Find your output sink name (the part before .monitor):

pactl list short sinks

Then wrap its monitor as a virtual source Chrome will list:

pactl load-module module-virtual-source \
    source_name=speakr_loopback \
    master=<sink_name>.monitor

Reload PXE MeetingMitra's input-device picker. You'll now see speakr_loopback in the dropdown. Pick it as "Also mix in" alongside your real mic and you'll capture both.

To persist across reboots, add the load-module module-virtual-source source_name=speakr_loopback master=<sink_name>.monitor line to ~/.config/pulse/default.pa.

PipeWire (newer distros)

Same idea. Wrap the monitor in a virtual source so Chrome doesn't filter it. Use pw-loopback: pw-loopback --capture-props='node.target=<sink>.monitor' --playback-props='media.class=Audio/Source node.name=speakr_loopback'. Or use qpwgraph / helvum to wire it visually.

Privacy notes

Most of these capture modes use standard browser APIs and have the same privacy properties as any other web app that records audio. A couple have specific trade-offs worth knowing.

Microphone, System Audio, Mic + System

All gated by the browser's permission system. The browser shows an explicit prompt before capture starts, displays a persistent recording indicator while it's running, and lets you revoke access at any time from the site settings.

Disable echo cancellation, noise suppression & auto-gain

These are audio-quality processors, not privacy controls. With them off the recording captures more of the ambient room (background voices, typing, notifications).

Virtual audio devices (BlackHole, VB-Cable, speakr_loopback, etc.)

This applies on every OS that uses a virtual audio device to route system audio into the browser. Once such a device exists on your machine, it shows up in navigator.mediaDevices.enumerateDevices() alongside your real microphone. Any site you've granted mic permission to can pick it and capture whatever audio is flowing through it, with the same "recording" indicator the browser shows for normal mic capture, not the "screen sharing" indicator.

Per-OS specifics:

  • macOS (BlackHole / Loopback). The device is a permanently installed audio driver. The risk is active only while system audio is actually routed through it, i.e. while your output is set to the Multi-Output Device you configured. When you're not recording, switch the system output back to your normal device so nothing flows through BlackHole.
  • Windows (VB-Cable, Voicemeeter, Stereo Mix). Same pattern. The risk is active while audio is routed through the cable. Native "Share system audio" via the share dialog has no comparable exposure because nothing is routed through a virtual mic.
  • Linux (speakr_loopback). Chrome's filtering of "Monitor of …" sources is a deliberate privacy protection. The module-virtual-source wrap explicitly undoes that protection. Unlike macOS/Windows the device is a runtime PulseAudio module you can load and unload at will.
Mitigations (apply on every OS):
  • Route system audio through the virtual device only while recording. On macOS, switch the system output back to your speakers / headphones after. On Windows, stop routing audio through the cable. On Linux, pactl unload-module module-virtual-source after, or don't put the load-module line in default.pa.
  • Audit your mic permissions at chrome://settings/content/microphone and remove any site that doesn't strictly need it.
  • Use a less obvious device name where you can choose one (e.g. aux_in instead of speakr_loopback on Linux). Doesn't stop a determined enumerator but raises the bar.
Where the audio ends up

Captured audio goes to your PXE MeetingMitra server (self-hosted by you) and then to whichever ASR endpoint you've configured. If you're running a local Whisper or self-hosted ASR, audio never leaves your network. If your ASR is OpenAI or another cloud provider, audio gets sent there.
